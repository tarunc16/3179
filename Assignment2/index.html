Are rich countries to blame for our climate woes?

The short answer is yes. The graph below maps the energy intensity per capita against the GDP per capita. The size of the bubbles correspond to the relative CO2 emissions per 100,000 individuals. Wealthier countries generally use more energy per individual. Correspondigly they also emit more emissions per individual. However, wealthier countries are also much better placed to invest in cleaner and greener renewable energy, and rapidly decarbonise their energy markets.

What does Australia's energy production look like?

Australia is one of the largest emitters of CO2 per capita in the world. This is largely due to the heavy reliance of Australian energy production on coal and natural gas. Coal is by far the dirtiest of them all, but Australia is well endowed with coal reserves making it a easier and cheaper to burn it. Natural gas generally emits 50% less CO2 than coal but is still a heavy emitter and also very expensive.

With teh abundance of sunlight, land and wind resources, Australia has the potential to be a renewable energy superpower. The reality is much more depressing however. Renewables still contribute a tiny amount to the total energy production. In recent years, renewables are becoming cheaper and hence more popular.

Are renewables the way forward?

There is finally broad consensus that pur energy markets need to be decarbonised and the world needs to embrace reneawable energy. There are many forms of renewables; solar energy, onshore wind, offshore wind, biogas, hydrogen.... Each jurisdiction must choose which form best suits their geography, electricity grid, and environment. Nations around the world are adopting these these technologies quickly. However, are we too late?

Nuclear energy is also a clean and green source of energy but there are serious saftey and health concerns surrounding it, especially after the Fukushima reactor meltdown in Japan.

Is there hope for the future?

To save our planet from catastrophic climate change we must limit global warming to 1.5 degrees by 2100. To do that we must rapidly decarbonise our energy markets and economies.

The OECD is a group of largely wealthy countries. The heatmap below shows the CO2 emissions per capita (tonnes) for each OECD member state from 1981 to 2019. We can see a growing emissions till about 2008. That seems to be the peak. Emissions have since come down and seems to be around 1980s level today. This is good news!

Ofcourse this does not include some of teh world's heaviest emitters like China and India. But both these nations have comitted to strong climate change action!
